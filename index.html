In early 2024, I made it a goal for myself to 1) finally learn how to write an html and css file from start to finish, and 2) get better at making artifacts that facilitate product decision making.

To put these skills to the test, I’ve decided to combine these goals into one and create a showcase of some of the artifacts that I’m proudest of, in no particular order.

Check out the things I made in 2024!!!

User Research Findings

For Learning Platform Rebuild – A slide deck that breaks down the methodology and findings of a focus group session held at our Irvine Campus.

Why I made it

“We’ve never tested this before, and I’m worried about ...”

Our previous research session had never gotten the visibility it deserved, and didn’t help our stakeholders feel reassured about the efficacy of my design proposals.

What made it click

My audience didn’t participate in or get any context on the user research effort.

I realized that it’d help them trust the data I’m communicating more if I explained the methodology and demographic background of our participants.

Impact

This deck was able to generate asynchronous discussion about user story we hadn’t heavily considered! While we aren’t taking action now, this gives context to metrics we should keep an eye on when we launch.

UI Kit (for a future design system)

For Learning Platform Rebuild – A robust Figma file that defines a new token system and hosts a library of components that transform with theme

Why I made it

In our legacy system rebuild, our developers were inundated with so many complex problems that long term, front end flexibility was a tough burden to shoulder.

As somewhat of a design system fanatic, I took it upon myself to create the starting draft of a theme-shifting token system and consolidated a year’s worth of my own decision making.

What made it click

Developer and design experience first – every time I observed a designer or a developer use Figma, I took lots of notes on their habits and pain points as if it was a user research session.

Additionally, I spent a lot of time consulting a developer on responsible token practices and researched other design systems.

Impact

This UI kit served as the blueprint for CSS variables that our team uses and expedited the development process.

Developers no longer had to find common threads across multiple designs or plan out tokens, and could trust that what they built would work as it was defined.

Customer Journey Map

For Beast Academy Parent Experiences – A user journey flow chart that maps each step a parent will encounter on our site, both online and offline.

Why I made it

As I was doing my own discovery work on a broadly scoped project, other designers were getting onboarded onto adjacent projects.. quickly. I thought this would be a nice way for them to onboard onto their own faster-paced projects, while it also helped my own project out.

What made it work

Placing screenshots on each step of the flow helped product stakeholders build familiarity with the parent journey and its existing pain points.

Impact

Facilitated meaningful conversations, brought light to some really painful user flows, and more teams in the company want one of their own!!

Product Audit (with flows)

For Beast Academy Parent Experiences – An organized collection of screenshots that maps out where buttons on our product lead us.

Why I made it

I had an ever-growing hoard of screenshots that I was losing track of. Many buttons led to slightly different resulting screens, and it became impossible for me to remember what I’d discovered while exploring our current product.

What made it work

Arrows!!

On screens with lots of hidden, deceptive buttons, it was worth mapping out every single action and resulting reaction.

Impact

This artifact helped me realize how many modals we have, especially on a single screen!!! At some point, this became a map that I could use to scare us into doing something about that..

Wrapping up...

As the year wraps up, I’m still finding loose ends and lots of conversations that are probably getting lost in the noise. But as a product designer, I feel proud of contributing what I can to each of the initiatives I work on!

At the end of the day, generating artifacts feels like working through the design process. I’m still thinking about the end user (our project teams) and what problems we are facing that could be addressed with a solution I create.
